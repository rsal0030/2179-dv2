The Rise of Australia's Space Nation

From no space agency to the Moon in under a decade

In 1969, two radio telescopes in rural Australia helped beam the first Moon landing to 600 million people around the world. Yet for decades after, Australia had no space agency, no national strategy, and no real industry to speak of. That changed in 2018, and what followed is one of the quiet success stories of the Australian economy.

SECTION 01

A Nation Wakes Up to Space

Australia was a space pioneer long before it had a space agency. Its vast, clear skies and southern hemisphere location made it indispensable to NASA during the Space Race. But when the Cold War ended and funding dried up, Australia stepped back from the global stage — dismantling its space office in 1996 and leaving the sector without direction for over two decades

Australia wasn't alone in coming late to space, but its return in 2018 marked a sharp turning point.

Australia wasn't alone in coming late to space, but compared to the rest of the world, its arrival in 2018 was remarkably recent, and its ambitions since then have been anything but modest.

Australia went without a national space agency — from 1996 to 2018

The agency was just the beginning. What followed was the deliberate construction of an entire industry from the ground up.

SECTION 02

From Zero to Launch

When the Australian Space Agency opened its doors in 2018, the domestic space sector was small, fragmented, and largely invisible to most Australians. Eight years later, it employs tens of thousands of people, generates billions in revenue, and is attracting serious commercial investment from around the world.

The headline numbers tell a growth story, but the real texture of that growth is visible in the number of organisations participating in the sector

Australian space organisations by FY23 — the highest ever recorded

The industry isn't just growing in headcount and revenue. It's putting hardware into orbit, and the story of Australia's satellites is one of the most striking parts of the whole picture.

SECTION 03

Eyes in the Sky - Australia's Satellite Story

For most of its history, Australia depended entirely on satellites built and owned by other countries. If you used GPS to navigate, watched live sport via satellite, or received a weather forecast, you were relying on American, European, or Japanese infrastructure. Australia was a consumer of space, not a contributor. That is changing rapidly.

~1kg

The average mass of a modern Australian nanosatellite — down from hundreds of kilograms in the 1980s

It's not just the number of satellites that has changed. The satellites themselves are fundamentally different from what came before.

The shift in what satellites weigh reflects a deeper change in what they are built for.

Where a satellite goes shapes everything it can do. The choice of orbit determines cost, lifespan, and capability, and that choice has shifted dramatically.

These satellites don't operate in isolation. Behind every object in orbit is a network of ground infrastructure, and Australia's geography gives it a natural advantage that most countries simply cannot replicate.

SECTION 04

Australia's Secret Superpower - Location

Ask a space engineer why Australia matters and they'll often start with a map. The continent's position in the southern hemisphere, its vast unpopulated interior, its stable political environment, and its clear skies make it one of the most strategically valuable pieces of real estate in the global space economy. You can't move a country, and Australia's location is increasingly worth billions.

1 of 3

NASA deep space tracking stations on Earth are located in Australia — at Tidbinbilla, ACT

This infrastructure isn't just supporting today's missions. It is being built and expanded with the next decade firmly in mind, because Australia's most ambitious space chapters are still unwritten.

SECTION 05

Shooting for the Moon and Beyond

Australia's Civil Space Strategy set bold targets for 2030: triple the sector's revenue to $12 billion, create 20,000 new jobs, and establish Australia as a recognised destination for orbital launch. When those targets were announced in 2019, they seemed aspirational. In 2025, with a lunar rover under construction, an Australian astronaut assigned to a mission, and commercial rockets launching from Australian soil, they are starting to look like a floor rather than a ceiling.

2030

Australia's target year to land the Roo-ver on the Moon as part of NASA's Artemis program

Behind these missions is an ecosystem of organisations, government agencies, universities, startups, and multinationals, all operating across different parts of the space value chain.

Australia entered the space age late. But in the seven years since the Australian Space Agency opened its doors at Lot Fourteen in Adelaide, the country has launched satellites, built rockets, signed agreements with NASA and ESA, and committed to putting a rover on the Moon. The next chapter in Australia's space journey is orbital launches from Australian soil, an Australian on the ISS, a growing commercial sector, is already underway. The giant leap has begun.
