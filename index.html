The Rise of Australia's Space Nation

From no space agency to the Moon in under a decade

In 1969, two radio telescopes in rural Australia helped beam the first Moon landing to 600 million people around the world. Yet for decades after, Australia had no space agency, no national strategy, and no real industry to speak of. That changed in 2018 — and what followed is one of the quiet success stories of the Australian economy.

A Nation Wakes Up to Space

Australia was a space pioneer long before it had a space agency. Its vast skies and southern hemisphere location made it essential to NASA during the Space Race. But by 1996, Australia had dismantled its space office and stepped back from the global stage.

Australia wasn't alone in coming late to space — but its return in 2018 marked a sharp turning point.

Australia’s return to space accelerates sharply after 2018, marking a structural shift from inactivity to sustained national investment.

Compared to global peers, Australia is a recent entrant — but it has moved quickly to build capability.

Australia is one of the newest space nations globally, yet is among the fastest to develop commercial and civil capability after entry.

From Zero to Launch

When the Australian Space Agency opened in 2018, the sector was small and fragmented. Today, it is a rapidly growing industry generating billions in revenue and employing thousands of Australians.

Employment grows steadily while revenue accelerates unevenly, suggesting a shift toward high-skill, knowledge-intensive roles rather than industrial scaling.

Beyond headline growth, the number of organisations reveals deeper industry expansion.

The sector expands rapidly post-2018, with COVID-19 causing only a temporary slowdown before record-high participation in FY23.

Eyes in the Sky — Australia's Satellite Story

Australia has shifted from relying entirely on foreign satellites to building and launching its own. This transition reflects both technological change and commercial growth.

Satellite launches cluster heavily after 2018, with more satellites launched in five years than in the previous several decades combined.

Satellite design has changed dramatically — becoming smaller and more accessible.

Satellite miniaturisation reshapes access to space, reducing cost barriers and enabling universities and startups to participate

These satellites rely on a global network of launch providers.

Australia’s reliance on international launch providers highlights both global integration and the absence of domestic launch capability.

Orbit selection reveals the shift toward modern low-cost satellite systems.

Low Earth Orbit becomes the dominant destination, reflecting global trends toward cheaper, faster, and more agile satellite systems.

Australia's Secret Superpower

Geography gives Australia a unique advantage in global space infrastructure, making it a critical hub for tracking, communication, and launches.

Heavier satellites cluster in higher, stable orbits while modern lightweight satellites concentrate in low Earth orbit, reflecting a structural shift in design philosophy.
